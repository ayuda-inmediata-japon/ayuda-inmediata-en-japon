🌐 Basic Guide in English

Simple and practical information for people who need help while traveling, living or staying in Japan.

🇯🇵 Japan help guide in English

This page gives basic guidance for common situations in Japan: arrival, police, transport, health, accommodation, money, daily life and procedures.

If you need help in Spanish, you can return to the main guide. For general questions, use the General Orientation button below.

📚 Main topics

✈️ Arrival in Japan

After landing, follow the signs for Arrivals / Immigration / Baggage Claim. Most airports in Japan are well organized and have English signs.

  • Prepare your passport and arrival documents.
  • Follow airport staff instructions calmly.
  • Check the baggage belt number for your flight.
  • Before leaving the airport, prepare internet, transport and your hotel address.

Useful phrase:

Where is immigration?

Japanese: 入国審査はどこですか？

Romaji: Nyuukoku shinsa wa doko desu ka?

🚓 Police / Koban

If you are lost, had an accident, lost something or need help, you can go to a police box called Koban.

  • For emergencies, call 110.
  • For lost items, ask at a Koban or station office.
  • If you lose your passport, contact your embassy and the police.

Useful phrase:

I need help.

Japanese: 助けてください。

Romaji: Tasukete kudasai.

🚆 Transport

Japan has trains, buses, taxis, airport buses, Shinkansen and IC cards such as Suica or Pasmo.

  • Use Google Maps or station signs to check platforms.
  • IC cards are useful for trains, buses and convenience stores.
  • Taxi doors usually open automatically.
  • For long trips, check Shinkansen tickets in advance.

Useful phrase:

How do I get to this station?

Japanese: この駅へはどう行きますか？

Romaji: Kono eki e wa dou ikimasu ka?

🏥 Health / Hospital

If you feel sick, look for a clinic, hospital or pharmacy. In emergencies, call 119 for ambulance or fire department.

  • Bring your health insurance card if you have one.
  • Some hospitals may require appointment or referral.
  • For minor symptoms, pharmacies can help with OTC medicine.
  • If serious, call 119 or ask nearby staff for help.

Useful phrase:

I feel sick.

Japanese: 具合が悪いです。

Romaji: Guai ga warui desu.

🏨 Accommodation

Japan has hotels, business hotels, capsule hotels, guesthouses, minshuku and internet cafés. Always keep your accommodation address in Japanese.

  • Check-in time is often after 3:00 PM.
  • Some hotels ask for passport or residence card.
  • Keep the hotel name and phone number saved.
  • If you arrive late, contact the hotel in advance.

Useful phrase:

I have a reservation.

Japanese: 予約があります。

Romaji: Yoyaku ga arimasu.

💴 Money / ATM

Cash is still useful in Japan. Many convenience stores have ATMs that accept international cards.

  • 7-Eleven, Lawson and FamilyMart often have ATMs.
  • Check withdrawal fees before using the ATM.
  • Some small restaurants may accept cash only.
  • Keep some coins for lockers, buses or vending machines.

Useful phrase:

Where is an ATM?

Japanese: ATMはどこですか？

Romaji: ATM wa doko desu ka?

🏠 Daily Life

Daily life in Japan has many rules and customs. Convenience stores, stations and city offices are very useful.

  • Separate trash according to local rules.
  • Be quiet on trains and avoid phone calls inside trains.
  • Convenience stores are useful for food, ATM, tickets and payments.
  • Always check signs and follow local manners.

Useful phrase:

Can you help me?

Japanese: 手伝ってもらえますか？

Romaji: Tetsudatte moraemasu ka?

📄 Procedures / City Hall

For residence, address registration, national health insurance, pension or certificates, you usually need to go to city hall.

  • Bring your residence card, passport and My Number if needed.
  • Ask staff which counter you need.
  • Some procedures may require Japanese forms.
  • If you do not understand, ask for simple Japanese or translation support.

Useful phrase:

I do not understand Japanese well.

Japanese: 日本語がよく分かりません。

Romaji: Nihongo ga yoku wakarimasen.

💡 Important advice:

Save your hotel address, emergency contacts, passport photo and important documents on your phone. In Japan, written information helps a lot when asking for assistance.
